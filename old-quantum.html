Old quantum theory → matrix & wave mechanics (1925–1926)

The Quantum Transition (1925–1926)

This section bridges the heuristic “old quantum” era, defined by Bohr–Sommerfeld quantization, to the rigorous foundations of modern mechanics. In the pivotal years of 1925–1926, two distinct yet mathematically equivalent frameworks emerged: Heisenberg’s matrix mechanics and Schrödinger’s wave mechanics.

While matrix mechanics focused on observable transitions, wave mechanics introduced the time-dependent Schrödinger equation to govern the system's evolution. Together with Born’s rule for probabilistic interpretation, these theories completed the shift from classical orbits to quantum states.

Detailed explorations of foundational milestones, such as blackbody radiation, the photoelectric effect, atomic spectra, the Bohr model, Compton scattering, de Broglie matter waves, and the heat capacity models of Einstein and Debye, are available on their respective pages.

Old Quantum (Bohr–Sommerfeld) Quantization

The Old Quantum Theory describes the era between 1900 and 1925 when physicists attempted to correct classical mechanics using ad hoc quantum rules. This period was defined by the transition from Newtonian physics to a deeper understanding of the subatomic world, primarily focused on explaining the stability and spectra of atoms.

The foundation of this era was the Bohr Model (1913). Niels Bohr proposed that electrons move in stationary states or orbits around the nucleus. He postualted that angular momentum is quantized in integer multiples of $\hbar$. This meant electrons could only occupy specific discrete paths, and energy was only released or absorbed as a photon when an electron "jumped" between these levels.

In 1916, Arnold Sommerfeld expanded this model to account for the complex fine structure of spectral lines. He introduced elliptical orbits and the Wilson–Sommerfeld quantization rule, which stated that the action integral for any periodic system must be an integer multiple of Planck’s constant ($h$). This introduced additional quantum numbers for the shape and orientation of orbits, significantly improving the model's accuracy for hydrogen-like atoms.

A guiding principle of this time was the Correspondence Principle, which dictated that quantum behavior must merge with classical physics at large scales or high energy levels. However, the theory had severe limitations. It could not explain the Helium atom (which has two electrons), nor could it predict the intensity of spectral lines.

Ultimately, the Old Quantum Theory was a hybrid of classical trajectories and arbitrary quantum constraints. Its internal contradictions led directly to the development of Matrix Mechanics and Wave Mechanics in 1925, where the concept of physical orbits was replaced by probability amplitudes and wavefunctions.

Bohr–Sommerfeld selects discrete orbits by the action condition $\oint p\,dq = n h$ (with integer $n$). For hydrogen this gives $r_n \propto n^2$ and $E_n \propto -1/n^2$. This explains the Balmer series and sets the stage for the 1925–1926 breakthroughs.

Matrix Mechanics (Heisenberg, Born, Jordan 1925)

In a radical departure from visual intuition, Werner Heisenberg abandoned the concept of electron orbits entirely. He replaced classical trajectories with tables of "transition quantities," which Max Born and Pascual Jordan identified as matrices. This shift moved physics away from what could be seen and toward what could be measured: discrete spectral frequencies and intensities.

In this framework, physical observables like position ($\hat{x}$) and momentum ($\hat{p}$) are no longer simple numbers, but operators that do not commute. This is captured by the fundamental canonical commutation relation:

$[\hat{x}, \hat{p}] = i\hbar$

By diagonalizing the Hamiltonian matrix, physicists could derive energy levels directly from the math. While the abstraction was initially daunting, Wolfgang Pauli famously validated the theory by applying it to the hydrogen atom, proving that this "matrix algebra" perfectly predicted the observed Rydberg states. Today, this remains the basis for the Heisenberg picture of quantum dynamics, where measurement outcomes correspond to the eigenvalues of these matrices.

Schrödinger’s wave mechanics (1926)

In 1926, Erwin Schrödinger reformulated quantum theory as a continuous wave problem. Instead of discrete jumps between orbits, he proposed that a particle is described by a complex valued wavefunction $\psi(\mathbf{r},t)$ evolving according to:

$$ i\hbar\,\partial_t\psi(\mathbf{r},t) = \hat{H}\psi(\mathbf{r},t), \qquad \hat{H}= -\frac{\hbar^2}{2m}\nabla^2 + V(\mathbf{r}). $$

This equation is structurally analogous to classical wave equations, but with a crucial difference: $\psi$ does not represent a physical wave like water or sound. Its squared magnitude, $|\psi|^2$, gives a probability density (Born interpretation).

For time independent potentials, solutions separate as: $$ \psi(\mathbf{r},t)=u(\mathbf{r})e^{-iEt/\hbar}, \quad \hat{H}u = E u. $$ This converts the problem into an eigenvalue equation. For hydrogen, where $V(r) = -\frac{e^2}{4\pi\varepsilon_0 r}$, the solutions take the form:

$$ u_{nlm}(\mathbf{r}) = R_{nl}(r)\,Y_{lm}(\theta,\phi), \qquad E_n = -\frac{13.6\,\text{eV}}{n^2}. $$

These solutions replace Bohr’s circular orbits with orbitals: spatial probability distributions determined by quantum numbers $(n,l,m)$. The electron is no longer a particle following a path, but a distributed probability amplitude constrained by boundary conditions.

The origin of this formulation lies in de Broglie’s hypothesis that matter has wave like properties ($\lambda = h/p$). Schrödinger extended this idea by connecting it to the Hamilton–Jacobi formalism, effectively replacing classical trajectories with wave phases $S$ through $\psi \sim e^{iS/\hbar}$.

The personal and intellectual contrast with Werner Heisenberg is striking. In 1925, Heisenberg developed matrix mechanics, a formulation that abandoned visualization entirely and focused only on observable quantities. His approach was algebraic, abstract, and conceptually radical, built on non commuting quantities like $[x,p]=i\hbar$.

Schrödinger, by contrast, resisted this abstraction. He sought a theory that preserved continuity and visual intuition. Where Heisenberg eliminated trajectories, Schrödinger replaced them with waves. Where Heisenberg used matrices, Schrödinger used differential equations. Despite their radically different appearances, the two formulations were soon proven to be mathematically equivalent.

Ironically, although Heisenberg’s formulation came first and was more fundamentally aligned with the observable nature of quantum mechanics, Schrödinger’s version became the dominant language of physics. The reason is practical: differential equations are solvable, visualizable, and directly connected to spatial intuition. Orbitals can be plotted; eigenvalues can be computed; boundary conditions can be imposed naturally.

In modern terms, both approaches describe the same underlying structure: a Hilbert space of states with operators acting upon them. Schrödinger’s equation corresponds to a specific representation where position is explicit and momentum becomes $\hat{p} = -i\hbar\nabla$.

The result is one of the most powerful frameworks in physics: a theory where reality is encoded not in definite trajectories, but in evolving probability amplitudes, deterministic in evolution, yet intrinsically probabilistic in measurement.

The Born Rule, Operators, and Expectation Values

The bridge between the abstract wave function and physical reality is the Born Rule. It dictates that the square of the magnitude of the wave function, $\rho(\mathbf{r},t) = |\psi(\mathbf{r},t)|^2$, represents the probability density of finding a particle at a specific point in space. To ensure physical consistency, the wave function must be normalized, meaning the total probability across all space must equal unity: $\int |\psi|^2\,d^3r = 1$.

In this framework, physical observables (such as position, momentum, or energy) are represented by Hermitian operators ($\hat{A}$). We cannot predict a single definitive outcome for a measurement; instead, we calculate the Expectation Value ($\langle \hat{A}\rangle$), which is the statistical average of many measurements performed on identically prepared systems:

$\langle \hat{A}\rangle = \int \psi^* \hat{A} \psi \, d^3r$

The dynamics of these averages are governed by Ehrenfest's Theorem, showing how quantum expectations evolve toward classical laws. The time evolution of an expectation value is determined by the commutator of the operator with the Hamiltonian ($\hat{H}$):

$\frac{d}{dt}\langle \hat{A}\rangle = \frac{i}{\hbar}\langle [\hat{H}, \hat{A}] \rangle + \langle \partial_t \hat{A} \rangle$

This relation demonstrates that if an operator commutes with the Hamiltonian and has no explicit time dependence, its expectation value is a constant of motion, representing a conserved quantity in the quantum system.

Heisenberg Uncertainty Principle (1927)

The most famous consequence of quantum mechanics is the Heisenberg Uncertainty Principle. It states that for any quantum state, the product of the uncertainties in position ($\Delta x$) and momentum ($\Delta p_x$) must satisfy the inequality:

$\Delta x\,\Delta p_x \ge \frac{\hbar}{2}$

More generally, for any two non-commuting operators ($\hat{A}$) and ($\hat{B}$), the Robertson Uncertainty Relation applies:

$\Delta A\,\Delta B \ge \frac{1}{2}|\langle [\hat{A},\hat{B}]\rangle|$

Crucially, this is a kinematic consequence of the wave-like nature of matter and the non-commutative algebra of operators. It is not a measurement disturbance artifact or a result of "clumsy" equipment. Instead, it represents a fundamental limit to the precision with which certain pairs of physical properties can be simultaneously defined.

If the commutator of two observables is non-zero, they are incompatible; the more precisely one is known, the more the other is "smeared" across the probability density.
