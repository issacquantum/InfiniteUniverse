About Me

I was born on July 6, 1992, in Mexico City, Mexico. I am Mexican.

I was raised in an urban environment under modest economic conditions, spending extended periods in a small 4x4 room within a tenement shared with members of my maternal family. My early family life was shaped by a turbulent parental relationship, which had a lasting influence on our household environment and on both my brother and me. Following my parents’ separation during childhood, my brother and I were raised primarily by our father.

My father began working at a young age as a blacksmith and developed into a highly skilled master in metalwork and fabrication. He has a strong aptitude for mechanical systems and the ability to analyze, repair, and construct complex devices independently. Over the years, he has worked across various roles, including positions in banking, retail, and private sector services. He emphasized discipline, fairness, and respect, and played an active role in my upbringing. My maternal background includes experience in music and hands-on technical skills, particularly in mechanical and electronic repair.

I received my early education in both public and private institutions. At approximately nine years old, I was enrolled in a private school, where I was introduced to English and a more rigorous academic environment. Despite strong academic performance, I have always experienced persistent social difficulties and developed an early aversion to conventional schooling structures. I continued through secondary education and later attended Vocational School No. 7 at the National Polytechnic Institute (IPN), eventually leaving formal education to enter the workforce with a clear orientation toward information technology.

From an early age, I demonstrated accelerated learning patterns, often grasping material quickly without the need for extensive study. My academic performance was consistently strong, though I've always shown little interest in routine tasks such as homework. My attention has always been highly selective, driven primarily by intrinsic interest rather than external structure.

When I was a baby, I remembered being in my dad's arms and, for the first time, hearing a piano. I did not know what a piano was, nor where the sound was being emanated from, probably a radio. Later, in my mid-teens, I found that song, which was identified as Sergei Rachmaninoff’s Rhapsody on a Theme of Paganini, Op. 43, Variation 18. That moment established a lasting connection to music and to pattern-based perception, shaping the way I interpret and engage with the world. The unique process of identifying this specific piece of music is detailed further in a subsequent section of this biography.

My interest in complex systems also emerged through early exposure to video games, particularly Tekken 3. I approached this not merely as entertainment, but as a deeply intricate, structured system of mechanics and strategy. This inclination toward analyzing systems extended naturally into my later interests in technology and computing.

I developed proficiency in both guitar and piano through self-directed study, demonstrating an accelerated learning curve. Initial functional competency in guitar was achieved within hours at age fifteen, and a similar rapid acquisition occurred for piano at age twenty-five. My approach relies on pattern recognition, auditory memory, and structural analysis rather than formal instruction.

Music is an internal, continuous process for me, often manifesting as original sequences that I can later reproduce specifically on piano and guitar. I am able to learn and perform complex pieces by ear on both instruments, reflecting a consistent ability to rapidly internalize and apply underlying systems.

My musical perspective has been shaped by composers such as Sergei Rachmaninoff and Johann Sebastian Bach, as well as technically complex modern artists including Animals as Leaders, Megadeth, Thank You Scientist, and An Endless Sporadic.

My engagement with technology began in childhood through early computer education and continued through independent exploration of systems, configurations, and devices. This sustained curiosity developed into a professional orientation toward IT and systems engineering, where I apply a consistent analytical and problem-solving approach.

Cognitively, I am highly observant and analytical, with a strong capacity for pattern recognition and the ability to process multiple lines of thought simultaneously. I retain detailed auditory and visual information after limited exposure. My attention is highly focused when engaged, though difficult to sustain in areas that lack intrinsic interest. I prefer solitary environments that allow for deeper concentration and analytical work.

My daily activities consistently integrate my primary interests, including programming, music (piano and guitar), and interactive systems such as Tekken. I maintain a relatively low sleep cycle while sustaining consistent energy levels.

Philosophically, my perspective centers on the nature of consciousness and the structure of reality. I conceptualize existence as an interconnected system in which individual entities are expressions of a broader, unified whole. My interests extend into physics, cosmology, and the theoretical understanding of time, complexity, and the universe.

My relationship with my father remains a central and defining element in my life, marked by its profound impact despite infrequent direct communication. Along with my brother, he is one of the few individuals I can say I love with all my heart, reflecting a deep sense of continuity, influence, and long-term impact.

This context underpins the work presented throughout this site, informing both my technical pursuits and my ongoing exploration of complex systems across diverse disciplines.

Music & Memory

The fragment that led me to Rachmaninoff

One of my earliest memories is not visual, but auditory.

As a baby, I heard only a few seconds of a piano piece, most likely from a radio somewhere. At the time, I had no concept of what a piano was, nor any understanding of where the sound originated. It appeared, existed briefly, and then vanished when the station changed, I guess, since other type of music was played instead. The moment was incomplete, almost accidental, yet it imprinted in my brain with unusual permanence.

I never forgot it.

That fragment remained with me as I grew, as I began to understand music, and as I tried, without any reference points, to find it again. I did not know the name of the piece, nor the names of composers. Still, the memory persisted, precise and unchanged, as if it were waiting.

Years later, in my mid-teens, my father and I visited my grandmother. She chose to watch Somewhere in Time. Early in the film, the theme began to play, and immediately, without hesitation, I recognized it. Not as something new, but as something recovered. The same fragment I had carried since childhood had reappeared, intact.

The recognition was not gradual. It was absolute.

Later in the film, the piece appears again under different circumstances, still unnamed. It is only near the end, after the narrative has unfolded across time, that it is finally identified: Rachmaninoff’s Rhapsody on a Theme of Paganini.

Hearing the name did not define the moment; it completed it.

The experience felt less like discovering something external and more like arriving at something that had always been internally present. It carried a sense of resolution, quiet but overwhelming. A form of familiarity that did not need explanation.

The film itself introduced another layer through the presence of the pocket watch, a closed loop in time, passed forward and backward between the same two individuals without origin. That paradox aligned with a long-standing preoccupation I had already developed: the structure of time, its non-linearity, and the possibility of self-contained systems without a clear beginning.

The piece did more than remain with me; it redirected me.

It became the central reason I chose to learn piano.

To this day, I have not yet learned Variation 18 itself. Instead, I began elsewhere within Rachmaninoff’s work, learning pieces such as the Élégie in E-flat minor, Op. 3 No. 1 and the Prelude in C-sharp minor, Op. 3 No. 2—each one another step toward something that, in a way, I had already been listening to long before I understood it.

Accelerated Learning

My learning curve is highly compressed. At fifteen, I reached functional competency on the guitar within hours of first handling the instrument, relying on pattern recognition, auditory feedback, and a basic introductory guide. At twenty-five, I acquired a piano and, following a single two-hour introductory session, was able to learn a substantial section of a Beethoven work. I then continued independently through self-directed study, guided by auditory memory and structural analysis.

This pattern,rapid intake, decomposition into underlying rules, and immediate application,repeats consistently across domains, including software systems, technical devices, and complex game mechanics.

Musical backdrop

My musical development did not follow a linear path; it expanded outward.

What I listened to early on created a broad search space for my ear, one defined less by genre and more by exposure, contrast, and continuous exploration. It began with Nirvana, largely through playing Guitar Hero on PlayStation. Around the same time, on my father’s side, an uncle would play records by The Doors, introducing a very different sonic texture. My grandmother, in contrast, listened to Elvis Presley; “It’s Now or Never” remains the piece that stayed with me the most.

From there, the process became self-directed. I began buying CDs from street markets and listening widely, without a defined direction but with constant intent. The Doors, Nirvana, and Elvis coexisted while I continued searching, still without knowing it, for that piano fragment I had heard years earlier. That search gradually led me toward classical music. I began listening to radio stations that played piano and opera, and over time Frédéric Chopin became a central reference point.

When I first encountered Sergei Rachmaninoff, I was unaware that he had composed the piece I had been searching for. The first work of his that I heard was Élégie, and the response was immediate. His music resonated at a different level, more direct, more aligned with how I internally processed sound. From that point, his work began to replace Chopin as my primary reference.

In parallel, my exposure continued to expand. After Guitar Hero 3, I moved into heavier and more technically complex music such as: Megadeth, An Endless Sporadic, Steve Ouimette, Evile, Buckethead, and Tool. Around eighteen, that trajectory deepened further with artists such as Animals as Leaders, CHON, Guthrie Govan, and John Coltrane. In my twenties, I encountered Thank You Scientist, which added yet another layer of complexity and structure.

Rather than replacing one another, these influences accumulated. Each added a different dimension—structural, technical, emotional—forming a composite framework through which I listen, analyze, and understand music.
