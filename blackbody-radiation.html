Blackbody Radiation

A blackbody is an idealized physical system that absorbs all incoming electromagnetic radiation, regardless of wavelength or direction. When such an object is in thermal equilibrium, it re-emits radiation with a very specific pattern, namely a spectrum that depends only on its temperature $T$, and not on what it is made of or its geometry.

In practical terms, physicists approximate a blackbody using a hollow cavity with a tiny hole. Radiation entering the hole gets trapped inside, bouncing around until it is fully absorbed. The radiation that leaks back out behaves almost exactly like that of a perfect blackbody. The quantity used to describe this emission is the spectral energy density $u(\nu, T)$, which tells us how much energy is present at each frequency $\nu$ for a given temperature.

Careful experiments in the late 19th century, notably by Lummer and Pringsheim, and independently by Rubens and Kurlbaum, measured this distribution across frequencies. What they found was striking: the behavior of the radiation splits into two distinct regimes.

At low frequencies, the data follow the Rayleigh–Jeans law: $$ u(\nu,T) \simeq \frac{8\pi\nu^2}{c^3} k_B T. $$ In plain terms, this expression says that the energy density increases as the square of the frequency ($\nu^2$) and also grows proportionally with temperature. In other words, hotter objects emit more radiation overall, and higher frequencies contribute increasingly more, at least within this low-frequency regime.

However, at high frequencies, experiments show the opposite trend. The radiation does not keep growing; instead, it drops off very rapidly. This behavior is captured empirically by Wien’s law: $$ u(\nu,T) \simeq a\,\nu^3 e^{-b\nu/T}. $$ In simple language, this tells us that although the $\nu^3$ factor initially increases the energy at higher frequencies, the exponential term $e^{-b\nu/T}$ dominates and forces the energy to decay sharply. In effect, very high-frequency radiation is strongly suppressed.

This created a serious problem for classical physics. The Rayleigh–Jeans formula predicts $u(\nu,T) \propto \nu^2 T$, which implies that energy keeps increasing without bound as frequency increases. If we sum, or integrate, this over all frequencies, the total energy becomes infinite, which is clearly unphysical. This inconsistency is known as the ultraviolet catastrophe.

In straightforward terms, classical theory was predicting that hot objects should emit an infinite amount of energy, mostly concentrated at extremely short wavelengths, such as ultraviolet and beyond. This is not what experiments show. The root of the issue lies in the classical assumption of equipartition, which assigns the same average energy $k_B T$ to every possible mode of vibration, with no natural limit at high frequencies.

The resolution came in 1900, when Max Planck introduced a new formula that correctly reproduces the entire observed spectrum: $$ u(\nu,T)=\frac{8\pi h \nu^3}{c^3}\,\frac{1}{e^{h\nu/k_B T}-1}, \qquad U(T)=\int_0^\infty u(\nu,T)\,d\nu = a\,T^4. $$ This expression, known as Planck’s law, can be understood as follows.

At low frequencies ($h\nu \ll k_B T$), the exponential term can be approximated, and the formula reduces to the Rayleigh–Jeans result. This means it recovers the classical behavior where energy grows with $\nu^2$ and $T$.

At high frequencies ($h\nu \gg k_B T$), the exponential term becomes very large, making the denominator grow rapidly. As a result, the energy density drops off, reproducing Wien’s exponential suppression.

The second part, $$ U(T) = a\,T^4, $$ tells us that the total energy density emitted by a blackbody increases as the fourth power of its temperature. In simpler terms, if you double the temperature, the emitted energy increases by a factor of $16$, which shows a very steep dependence.

The key new ingredient introduced by Planck is the constant $h$, known as Planck’s constant. This sets a fundamental scale for when quantum effects become important.

Planck’s breakthrough idea was to assume that energy is not exchanged continuously, but in discrete packets: $$ E = h\nu. $$ In plain words, this means energy comes in indivisible units, each proportional to the frequency. Higher-frequency radiation corresponds to larger energy packets.

This simple assumption prevents the runaway accumulation of energy at high frequencies. Since high-frequency modes require larger energy quanta, they are harder to excite at a given temperature. This naturally suppresses the ultraviolet region of the spectrum.

Two immediate consequences follow from Planck’s theory.

First, the peak of the spectrum shifts with temperature according to Wien’s displacement law, $\nu_{\max} \propto T$. In simple terms, as an object gets hotter, the frequency at which it emits most strongly moves toward higher values, for example from infrared to visible light.

Second, the total radiated power follows the Stefan–Boltzmann law: $$ P = \sigma A T^4. $$ This means that the total energy emitted per unit time increases very rapidly with temperature, much faster than linearly. Even a modest increase in temperature leads to a dramatic increase in radiated power.
