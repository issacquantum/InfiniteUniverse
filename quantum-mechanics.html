Quantum Mechanics

The term quantum (from the Latin quantus, meaning “how much” or “how much of something”) refers to a discrete unit, that is, a smallest indivisible amount in which a physical quantity can be exchanged. In contrast to classical physics, where quantities are assumed to vary continuously, the quantum perspective states that certain properties come in fixed “packets” rather than arbitrary values.

The word mechanics denotes the branch of physics concerned with describing motion, change, and the evolution of physical systems over time. When combined, quantum mechanics becomes the theoretical framework that governs how nature behaves at microscopic scales, such as atoms, electrons, and photons, where classical intuitions no longer apply.

Within this framework, key physical quantities, including energy, angular momentum, and other measurable properties, called observables, do not take on arbitrary values. Instead, they appear in discrete sets under many circumstances. In simple terms, systems are often restricted to specific allowed values rather than a continuous range.

The state of a quantum system is described by a mathematical object called the wavefunction, denoted by $\psi$. This function encodes all the information that can be known about the system. However, it does not directly describe a physical quantity like position or velocity in the classical sense.

Instead, the quantity $$ |\psi|^2 $$ represents a probability density. In plain terms, this means it tells us how likely we are to find the system in a particular state or at a particular location when a measurement is performed. Higher values correspond to greater likelihood, while lower values indicate less probable outcomes.

This probabilistic interpretation marks a fundamental departure from classical physics. Quantum mechanics does not, in general, predict exact outcomes. Rather, it provides a structured way to calculate the likelihood of different possible results.

The evolution of the wavefunction over time is governed by deterministic equations, such as the Schrödinger equation. However, the results of measurements themselves are inherently probabilistic. In simple terms, the underlying dynamics follow precise mathematical rules, while what we observe is described in terms of probabilities.
