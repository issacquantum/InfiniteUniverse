Following the formation of neutral atoms during the epoch of recombination, approximately 380,000 years after the Big Bang, the universe entered a period known as the cosmic Dark Ages. During this interval, the cosmos was filled mainly with neutral hydrogen and helium gas, but no luminous objects yet existed to produce fresh visible light. The Cosmic Microwave Background (CMB (cosmic microwave background)) photons were already decoupled from matter and could travel freely, yet the first stars had not formed. This stage extended from about \( 3.8 \times 10^{5} \text{ years} \) to several hundred million years after the Big Bang.

The Dark Ages were therefore defined by the relative absence of visible and ultraviolet electromagnetic radiation. During this time, the existing matter, primarily neutral gas and dark matter, slowly began to gather under the influence of gravity. Small overdensities in the otherwise nearly uniform matter distribution, seeded by quantum fluctuations during the inflationary epoch, attracted additional matter as time passed. Those regions would eventually become dense enough to collapse and give rise to the first stars and galaxies.
