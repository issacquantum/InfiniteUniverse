Determining Cosmic Time

The age of the universe refers to the time elapsed since the Big Bang. According to the Lambda-CDM (Lambda cold dark matter) cosmological model, the most accepted scientific model describing the universe's evolution, the universe is currently estimated to be approximately \( 1.38 \times 10^{10} \text{ years} \) old. This precise age is derived from a combination of fundamental physics and precise astronomical observations, primarily by measuring the expansion rate of the universe and extrapolating backward to its origin.

Calculations from Cosmic Expansion

The primary method for calculating the age of the universe involves measuring the Hubble Constant \( (H_0) \), which quantifies the rate at which the universe is expanding today. If the universe has been expanding at a relatively constant rate, its age would simply be the inverse of the Hubble Constant \( (H_0) \):

$$t_H = \frac{1}{H_0}$$

This is a useful first estimate, but only a rough one, because the real universe has not expanded at a constant pace.

However, the expansion rate has not been constant throughout cosmic history. The universe's expansion has been influenced by its varying energy density (composed of matter, radiation, and dark energy). In the early universe, matter and radiation slowed the expansion due to gravity, while in recent cosmic epochs, dark energy has caused the expansion to accelerate. Therefore, a more sophisticated calculation derived from the Friedmann equations is necessary to accurately determine the age of the universe.

The age of the universe \( (t_0) \) is given by the integral of the inverse of the Hubble parameter \( H(z) \) over redshift \( z \):

$$t_0 = \int_{0}^{\infty} \frac{dz}{(1+z)H(z)}$$

For a flat \( \Lambda \)CDM (cold dark matter) model, \( H(z) \) can be expressed as:

$$H(z) = H_0 \sqrt{\Omega_m (1+z)^3 + \Omega_r (1+z)^4 + \Omega_\Lambda}$$

Precise measurements of these cosmological parameters and of \( H_0 \) are what make an accurate age calculation possible.

Observational Evidence

The most precise measurements of these cosmological parameters come from detailed studies of the Cosmic Microwave Background (CMB (cosmic microwave background)), particularly from missions like NASA (National Aeronautics and Space Administration)'s WMAP (Wilkinson Microwave Anisotropy Probe) and the European Space Agency's Planck satellite. The CMB provides a snapshot of the universe when it was only about \( 3.8 \times 10^{5} \text{ years} \), offering critical data on its early composition and expansion rate.

In practice, missions such as WMAP and Planck do not measure "age" as an isolated quantity. They measure the pattern of temperature fluctuations in the early Cosmic Microwave Background, infer the values of the main cosmological parameters, and then solve for the expansion history that best matches those observations. The resulting age is therefore the age of the cosmological model that fits the data most accurately.

Independent confirmation comes from observations of the oldest known objects, such as globular clusters (dense groupings of very old stars) and very distant galaxies. The ages derived for these objects are consistent with, and do not exceed, the cosmic age derived from CMB and Hubble Constant \( (H_0) \) measurements, providing a crucial consistency check for the ΛCDM model.
