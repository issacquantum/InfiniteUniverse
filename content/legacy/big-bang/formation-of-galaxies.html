From Cosmic Webs to Galactic Structures

The process of galaxy formation is deeply intertwined with the evolution of the universe following the Big Bang. It began during the cosmic Dark Ages, as gravity slowly amplified tiny density fluctuations that originated from the quantum realm during cosmic inflation. These regions of slightly higher matter density started to attract more dark matter and primordial gas (hydrogen and helium). Over hundreds of millions of years, these overdense regions collapsed to form the first gravitationally bound structures, which served as the seeds for galaxies.

This hierarchical process gives rise to the cosmic web, a large-scale pattern in which galaxies form inside the densest intersections of dark matter filaments. Because dark matter contributes roughly \( 27\% \) of the universe's total energy budget, it provides most of the gravitational scaffolding on these scales. The resulting dark matter halos acted as gravitational wells that pulled in and trapped baryonic matter, allowing visible galactic structures to emerge.

Once these halos became deep enough, gas did not simply fall inward and turn into stars automatically. It had to cool, lose pressure support, and redistribute angular momentum. Those steps mattered because they helped determine whether a forming system remained compact and chaotic, or whether it could settle into a rotating disk that would later resemble a more familiar spiral galaxy.

The First Galaxies

The earliest galaxies are thought to have formed around \( 3 \times 10^{8} \text{ to } 5 \times 10^{8} \text{ years} \), after the formation of the first stars (Population III stars). These initial protogalaxies were relatively small and irregular, born from the collapse of primordial gas clouds within dark matter halos. The Population III stars within these early clumps enriched the surrounding intergalactic medium with heavy elements through their explosive supernova deaths. This enrichment cooled the gas, making it easier for more stars to form and for the protogalaxies to grow.

The intense ultraviolet radiation from these first stars and quasars (powered by supermassive black holes in the centers of early galaxies) began the process of reionization. This epoch saw the neutral hydrogen gas in the universe being re-ionized, making the universe transparent to light and allowing photons to travel freely once again. This was a critical phase in galaxy evolution, as it marked the end of the cosmic Dark Ages.

Growth and Evolution of Galaxies

Over billions of years, galaxies grew through a combination of processes:

Accretion of gas: galaxies continuously drew in fresh gas from the intergalactic medium, fueling ongoing star formation.

Mergers and interactions: smaller galaxies frequently collided and merged to form larger systems. These mergers are a primary driver of galaxy growth and morphological transformation, helping produce the diversity of spiral, elliptical, and irregular galaxies observed today. Major mergers can also trigger intense bursts of star formation and feed central supermassive black holes.

Star formation and feedback: within galaxies, gas clouds collapse to form new stars. The energy released by supernovae and stellar winds from massive stars can heat and expel gas from galaxies, a process known as feedback, which regulates further star formation.

Supermassive black holes: most massive galaxies host a supermassive black hole at their center. The growth of these black holes and the galaxies themselves appears to be closely linked, with feedback from active galactic nuclei (AGN (active galactic nucleus)) influencing the star-formation history of their host galaxies.

Morphology therefore emerges from history, not from a single fixed template. Repeated mergers tend to randomize stellar orbits and help produce elliptical galaxies, while quieter accretion histories make it easier for a system to preserve or rebuild a disk structure. What we now classify as spiral, elliptical, or irregular is the visible record of how gas supply, collision history, feedback, and dark-matter environment interacted across cosmic time.

The formation of galaxies is an ongoing process. Current observations and simulations like those from the James Webb Space Telescope provide insights into the properties of these early, distant galaxies, helping us piece together the complete cosmic history.

This picture is still being refined. Webb has already revealed surprisingly luminous and chemically evolved systems at very early times, forcing cosmologists to tighten their models of star formation, assembly rate, and feedback in the first billion years. Galaxy formation is therefore not a solved story, but an active effort to reconcile observation, simulation, and fundamental cosmology.
