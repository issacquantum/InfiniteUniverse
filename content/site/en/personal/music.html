Overview

Music has never been separate from the way I think. Long before I studied physics, programming, or systems with any real structure, music was already teaching me about tension, pattern, proportion, repetition, and release. It is one of the few disciplines in which emotion and architecture coexist without contradiction. A phrase can be expressive, but it still has to hold together. A piece can feel free, but it still depends on form.

My strongest connection is to the piano, not only as an instrument, but as a space where thought can become audible. Composition, even when it begins from intuition, quickly becomes a problem of structure: voicing, movement, restraint, balance, and timing. That is part of why music remains so close to the rest of my work. It trains the same attention I rely on elsewhere, the need to hear when something is excessive, when something is unresolved, and when a pattern finally locks into coherence.

My relationship with music has never been based on fame, money, or external recognition. Music stays close to me through its structure, sound, and meaning. Guitar first became real to me through a schoolmate's instrument, then through the second-hand guitar my mother gave me and the Dean VMNT (Dave Mustaine VMNT signature guitar model) United Abominations electric guitar my father later bought. My connection to piano became even more meaningful when my father admitted that he had quietly wanted to play piano for years. Guitar and piano entered my life through different paths, but both became permanent parts of how I think, remember, and build.
