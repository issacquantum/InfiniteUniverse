Music, Memory, and Composition

I cannot remember exactly how little I was when my relationship with music began. I only know that I heard a fragment of piano music before I had the language to name it. That sound stayed with me. Years later, while watching Somewhere in Time, I recognized it: Rhapsody on a Theme of Paganini, Op. 43 by Sergei Rachmaninoff. That discovery made Rachmaninoff my favourite piano composer and tied music, memory, film, and time together in a way that still shapes me.

Guitar became serious when a schoolmate let me take his acoustic guitar home. It came with a magazine of basic chords, notation, and hand positions. Within days I understood that I could learn by ear and play what I heard in my mind. My mother later gave me my first guitar, and my father bought me a Dean VMNT (Dave Mustaine VMNT signature guitar model) United Abominations electric guitar connected to Megadeth. I no longer have it, but it remains my favourite electric guitar.

My guitar taste moved through metal, thrash, and rock, including The Doors, Metallica, Megadeth, and Nirvana. Later, the technical side of guitar opened toward music by Animals as Leaders, Thank You Scientist, An Endless Sporadic, and CHON (carbon, hydrogen, oxygen, and nitrogen). I am currently saving for an Abasi Concept 8-string guitar.

Piano became real later, when I finally acquired one at 25. It felt even more intuitive than I expected. The same ear-based learning transferred immediately, and piano became the instrument where I could improvise, compose, and make thought audible. After I bought it, my father told me he had always wanted to play piano himself, which made the instrument more personal.

My relationship with music has never been based on fame, money, or external recognition. Music stays close to me through structure, sound, memory, and composition. Guitar and piano entered my life through different paths, but both became permanent parts of how I think, remember, and build.
