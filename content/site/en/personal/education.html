Childhood Questions

My education began long before any classroom. My father is a deeply religious man, and when I was young he often told my brother and me biblical stories. I do not remember the exact questions I asked, but I remember that those stories brought the word God into my world. When I asked what God was, my father described God as something with no beginning and no end. That was one of the first times the idea of infinity entered my mind. I did not yet have the language to connect it to existence, time, or the universe, but the question stayed there. God did not feel real to me in the same way that being alive felt real, so I kept the doubt mostly private and began trying to understand existence on my own.

School, English, and Independent Study

My first experience with formal education was kindergarten, where I quickly grew bored. Other children my age did not seem to think the way I did. I saw them as younger, not out of arrogance, but because my questions and interests already existed in a different dimension from theirs. Things did not improve at the public elementary school I attended before my parents separated. I was bullied by classmates.

After my parents separated, my father, despite serious financial hardship, found a way to enroll my brother and me in Instituto Valle, a private institution. The academic environment was more challenging and more suited to how my mind worked. It was there that I learned to speak English, and where my favourite subjects, music, English, and mathematics, began to take shape. Outside of school, something else was quietly happening. My father kept a small encyclopedia at home. One day I picked up a book at random, drawn only by its color, and it turned out to be an astronomy book, filled with images of planets and outer space. I read sections I did not fully understand, encountering ideas far beyond my age. Because my father's worldview was religious, I pursued that curiosity quietly, conducting my own research without anyone in my family knowing. That was one of the first times education became something I did for myself, outside permission and outside the classroom.

Music, Memory, and Discipline

I cannot remember exactly how little I was when my relationship with music began. I only know that I still could not speak when I heard a fragment of piano music, likely from a radio. I had no concept of what a piano was, no name for what I was hearing. But that small fraction of sound stayed with me, embedded somewhere beneath conscious memory, for the rest of my life. As I grew older and learned what a piano was, I knew asking my father for one was not a realistic possibility given our circumstances. So I researched piano music on my own, searching without knowing what I was searching for, trying to recover that lost piece from that early memory. The way I did it was modest but determined. My father would occasionally give me between ten and twenty Mexican pesos, which I would save to buy copied CDs from the street market, where vendors sold music for almost nothing. Other times I would walk to the nearest internet café and use one of their computers to search for musicians and bands, navigating a world of sound I was only beginning to map. I did not find the lost piece then, but I did discover Chopin, and connected deeply with his music.

Guitar and the First Serious Commitment

At 13 to 14 years old, my father enrolled me in Watson & Crick, a private middle school, where my interests in music, mathematics, and independent learning continued to develop. By that age my musical taste had already evolved toward metal, thrash, and rock. Bands like The Doors, Metallica, Megadeth, and Nirvana formed the foundation of what I was listening to. Then, almost by accident, a schoolmate did not want to carry his acoustic guitar home one day and asked me to take it. At home, with little else around, I picked it up out of curiosity. The guitar came with a magazine that introduced basic chords, notation, and hand positioning. Within minutes it felt natural. Within days I had learned what most people take months or years to develop. I could play what was in my mind. I could learn pieces by ear. Guitar became my new addiction, and I made a decision I have held to this day: I would never stop playing. After that first borrowed instrument, my mother gave me a second-hand guitar of my own, and my father later bought me my first electric guitar: a Dean VMNT (Dave Mustaine VMNT signature guitar model) United Abominations model connected to Megadeth, still my favourite electric guitar even though I no longer have it. I have since reached a level where I can play works by Animals as Leaders, Thank You Scientist, An Endless Sporadic, and CHON (carbon, hydrogen, oxygen, and nitrogen), and I am currently saving to acquire an Abasi Concept 8-string guitar.

I later attended a Bellas Artes music school. Entering that environment was not difficult for me, but the institution did not become what I expected. Teachers were often absent, and when they did appear, the instruction felt brief, inconsistent, and unsatisfying. I learned far more on my own. Leaving that school did not mean I abandoned music; it confirmed that my path with music would be personal, self-directed, and permanent.

The Piano Piece I Had Been Searching For

It was also around age 15 that I finally recovered the piece I had been searching for since that early memory. Watching the film Somewhere in Time, I heard it again and recognized it immediately: Rhapsody on a Theme of Paganini, Op. 43 by Sergei Rachmaninoff. What struck me was that I had already been listening to Rachmaninoff without knowing it. It was his music that had reached me when I was too young to name it, and it was his music I had unknowingly returned to. He remains my favourite piano composer to this day. Somewhere in Time has also remained with me ever since, and not only because of the music. It is my favourite film of all time. The story is built around time travel, which has always been one of my deepest obsessions, and there is something that has never stopped feeling meaningful to me about the fact that the piece of music I had been searching for my entire life was waiting for me inside a film about the very subject that has consumed my curiosity since childhood. From that point on, music, memory, film, and self-directed learning felt tied together in a way I could not ignore.

Formal Schooling and Leaving IPN (Instituto Politécnico Nacional)

After middle school came preparatory school, where I enrolled at IPN (Instituto Politécnico Nacional), directed toward industrial engineering, though not entirely by my own choosing. My father steered that decision. The only classes I genuinely attended were mathematics, trigonometry, calculus, and informatics, and even then, attendance was not driven by the institution but by the rare individuals within it. The trigonometry teacher, who also taught mathematics and calculus, was the single educator there who demonstrated real interest in teaching, someone who made the material feel alive and worth showing up for. The informatics teacher was inconsistent at best, frequently absent without explanation, making it difficult to take the subject seriously regardless of my interest in computing. The rest of the curriculum offered nothing that challenged me. The classes were not difficult. The environment was not stimulating. I was not happy there, the truth is that even if financial necessity had not eventually forced me to leave, I would have left on my own terms. The school was not the right place for the way my mind worked, and I knew it early. Eventually, financial necessity made the decision formal, and I left IPN before completing my studies. Outside the institution, my independent study continued moving faster than the curriculum around me. By then, I had already learned that the subjects that mattered most to me would have to be pursued alone.

Computing Before Career

At 18, I began my independent path, relocating to Querétaro and living on my own for the first time. My interest in computing, which had grown steadily through my years at IPN, proved to be the skill that opened professional doors for me. But that interest did not begin at IPN. It began much earlier, at the first private school my father enrolled me in as a child, where I was introduced to computers for the first time and immediately felt a pull toward them that I could not fully explain then but have never questioned since. Something about the logic of it, the way a machine could be instructed, the relationship between a precise input and a precise output, spoke directly to the same part of my mind that was drawn to mathematics and structure. From that first encounter, the interest never paused. It grew every day, quietly and consistently, through every school I attended, through every hour I spent at internet cafés researching music and everything else my curiosity reached for, through my years at IPN where informatics was one of the few classes that held any relevance to the direction I already knew I was heading. By the time I arrived in Querétaro at eighteen, computing was not a skill I had recently acquired. It was something I had been building toward my entire life without anyone having to tell me to.

Piano and Composition

At 25, I finally acquired a piano. Something I had wanted since before I had the words to ask for it. What I did not anticipate was how natural it would feel. Piano proved to be even more intuitive for me than guitar. The same ability to learn by ear transferred immediately, and I found myself able to play what lived in my mind with a directness that guitar could not always offer. I can play works by Chopin and Rachmaninoff, the composers who had shaped my relationship with music long before I ever touched a key. Beyond interpreting the work of others, the piano became the instrument through which I improvise, compose, and give form to the music that has always existed inside my head. It was, in every sense, the instrument I had been waiting for my entire life.

After I bought that piano, my father admitted something he had never really shared with me before: he had always wanted to play piano. That made the instrument even more meaningful. Without knowing it, I had found my way toward something he had quietly carried within himself for years.

Independent Learning Pattern

Throughout all of it, one pattern has remained constant: I have always learned on my own terms, driven by genuine curiosity rather than institutional structure. Formal education, as it is commonly designed, rarely kept pace with the questions I was already carrying. The subjects that shaped me most were the ones I pursued privately, independently, and without permission. That has never changed. My education is not just a list of schools I attended. It is the record of how I learned to teach myself, how I learned to trust curiosity over permission, and how that habit became the foundation for music, technology, and the intellectual work I continue building today.

Programming Education and This Site

My programming education has not followed a conventional path. I study through freeCodeCamp, Eric Matthes's Python Crash Course, 3rd Edition: A Hands-On, Project-Based Introduction to Programming, multiple Udemy courses on HTML (HyperText Markup Language), CSS (Cascading Style Sheets), Java, and Python, Harvard CS50x 2024 and Harvard CS50x 2026, taught by Professor David J. Malan. I knew I wanted to create a website dedicated to myself and to specific people important to me when I was 25, and the idea has been taking shape little by little ever since. Earlier versions were limited by the computer I had at the time and by how much I still needed to learn about programming. Because time is limited, this website became the main place where I put programming into practice as I learn it. I do not always have time to test every concept in separate side projects, so the project itself became the laboratory.

Starting Over With Better Structure

Before this version, I had already built a similar site, but I eventually disliked it after finding too many mistakes and too much uncontrolled syntax mixing. I chose to start from scratch. That decision has worked because the current structure lets me improve sections in the right order without constantly breaking unrelated parts of the site. The serious rebuild is recent, but the larger project has been forming since I was 25, often requiring time away from work, music, Tekken, study, and other activities I care about. Necessity pushed me toward technology, but the goal has never been money for its own sake. I care about having the specific tools, stability, and resources that let me keep doing my central work: answering the questions I carry, making and studying music, playing Tekken, and programming. As I say elsewhere on this site, the resource I lack most is time. I cannot focus on only one activity, so I sometimes spend days or weeks away from a discipline, but I always return to it.
