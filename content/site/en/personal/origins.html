Childhood Home

I was born in Mexico City and grew up in a small room inside a tenement. The space was tiny, almost a complete home compressed into a single room, but it was there that many of the earliest parts of my identity began to form.

My childhood was shaped by complexity, limitation, and contrast. There was chaos around me, but there was also discipline, survival, and the need to pay attention. I learned early that the world outside me could be unstable, so I began building an inner world through reading, observation, imagination, and questions I did not yet know how to name.

Reading became one of the first ways I learned to access worlds beyond my immediate circumstances. It was not only an academic skill. It became a private opening into thought, language, memory, and distance from the limits of the room I grew up in.

Reading, PlayStation, and Early Systems

When I was around five or six years old, a reading challenge became one of the first moments where I understood discipline as something that could change my environment. I do not remember the title of the book or the exact pages I read, but I remember the challenge itself: I had to read a section of an adult book and explain what I understood.

That challenge led to my first PlayStation 1. What began as a reward for reading and discipline became my first deep encounter with digital systems, competition, reflexes, pattern recognition, and virtual worlds.

Through that PlayStation, my obsession with Tekken and Gran Turismo began. Those games were not just entertainment to me. They became worlds of mastery, precision, repetition, and identity. Tekken, especially, became one of the earliest places where I understood the feeling of serious competition.

Music and Practical Intelligence

Music entered my life early through the guitar. At first, I resisted it without understanding what I was missing. Later, receiving my first guitar and then my first electric guitar gave music a permanent place in my life.

I also grew up around practical intelligence: repairing, adapting, and making things work under pressure. That left a strong impression on me because it showed intelligence as something practical, immediate, and tied to survival.

Pressure, Sports, and Boundaries

My early life was shaped by pressure before I had the language to explain it. School, home, money, emotional weight, and uncertainty all became part of the same environment. My own interrupted education did not come from a lack of curiosity; it came from a life where survival, responsibility, and instability often moved faster than any formal plan.

As a child, I was drawn to basketball, soccer, and football, not just as things to watch but as things to practice. Sports became one early place where I learned to take effort, competition, timing, physical intelligence, and self-control seriously.

Around the age of six or seven, my home life changed in a way that added another layer of emotional weight to an already complex childhood. Over time, I had to make decisions about distance, boundaries, and what could remain healthy for me. Those decisions became part of how I learned to protect my inner world without denying where I came from.

Looking Back

Looking back, the early pattern is clear. Reading gave me access to thought. PlayStation introduced me to systems, competition, and digital worlds. Tekken and Gran Turismo gave mastery a form. Music stayed after those first instruments. Practical intelligence showed me how much pressure can shape attention. The environment I grew up in was small and difficult, but it forced me to build an inner world that was expansive and intense.
