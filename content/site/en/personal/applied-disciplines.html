How I Started Playing Tekken

It all started at age five with Tekken 2. It caught my attention, but it was Kazuya Mishima who truly pulled me in. His fighting style, his Devil transformation, and his iconic soundtrack (the very one playing in the background of this section).

Then came Tekken 3. I picked Jin Kazama (Kazuya and Jun's son) purely based on looks, but connected with his moveset, a fusion of Kazuya's raw aggression and Jun's defensive style. The shift to a full 3D movement system deepened everything: more complex mechanics, richer character expression. I became completely addicted, to the point of memorizing the full move list of every character, including secret moves.

Kazuya has been my main throughout the entire series. The only exception was Tekken 3, where his absence left me no choice but to play Jin. Yet even then, the connection remained, because Jin carried the same fighting DNA (deoxyribonucleic acid) that first pulled me toward Kazuya. Across every entry, Kazuya has preserved the core that defines him, while continuously expanding his arsenal with refined and evolved moves. That same depth and complexity lives on in Tekken 8. He remains one of the most technically demanding characters to pilot in the game, requiring precision, commitment and a deep understanding of his mechanics. His design, his story, his presence. To me, Kazuya Mishima is the greatest character in any video game ever made.

Tekken has been a constant throughout my life. I don't approach it as a casual game. I treat it as a serious discipline, the same way I approach the rest of my serious work. That means it isn't always fun, especially when losing. But that's part of it.

My favourite entries are Tekken 3, Tekken Tag Tournament, Tekken Tag Tournament 2, Tekken 7, and Tekken 8. I entered competitive online play with Tekken 7 and I'm still active in Tekken 8, though life keeps me busy between music, physics, programming, technical work, and more. I will play Tekken until the day I die.

Beyond Tekken, there are other video games that have stayed with me since I was around five or six, and they are joyful to me in a different way. Some I take seriously, some I play simply because they make me happy, but each one has its own place in my life.

I have played video games since the age of five, and I do not play many. I return to a small group of games because they mean something specific to me. The two that have defined my life the most are Tekken and Gran Turismo. These are not games to me. They are disciplines, approached with the same seriousness and commitment I bring to the rest of my serious work. The only titles I play purely for enjoyment are Grand Theft Auto V and Dragon Ball: Sparking Zero, and even then, my curiosity about the systems and physics underneath the experience is never fully switched off.

Gran Turismo 7 occupies a singular place among these. It is, without question, the racing game I take most seriously, both physically and emotionally. I value it for its demand for patience, precision, restraint, and repetition. Every lap feels like a conversation between the car, the track, and my own discipline. It gives me the same feeling I look for in the things I care about most: not just entertainment, but mastery.

Gran Turismo 7 also carries personal weight that exists entirely outside of physics. It is my father's favourite video game. That alone gives it a dimension no other title in my library possesses.

My vehicle of choice within Gran Turismo 7 is the Porsche 911 GT3 RS (Grand Touring 3 Rennsport). I am speaking from the game, not from real driving experience. I have never driven a real vehicle, but digitally the GT3 RS feels like the clearest version of what draws me to racing games: precision, tension, speed, and the constant need to control mistakes. In Gran Turismo 7, its rear-wheel drive makes every lap feel sharp, demanding, and easy to ruin if I get careless. That digital relationship is exactly what draws me to it.

It is not only my favourite vehicle in the game. Porsche is also my favourite car maker in real life, and one of my dreams is to own at least a Porsche Macan GTS (Gran Turismo Sport) one day. Their engineering standards are part of my attachment to the brand, and Gran Turismo 7 does real honor to Porsche.

My favourite circuit is the Nürburgring Nordschleife, and the reasons are both historical and technical. The Nordschleife, located in the Eifel region of Germany, was constructed between 1925 and 1927 and stretches over 20.8 kilometres of relentlessly varied terrain. It was designed not as a modern sanitized racing circuit but as a road carved through a forested mountain landscape, incorporating over 170 corners, dramatic elevation changes of up to 300 metres, blind crests, narrow sections, and surfaces that behave differently depending on weather, temperature, and time of day. It earned its name as the Green Hell, a title given to it by the late Sir Jackie Stewart, one of the greatest Formula 1 drivers in history, who considered it the most dangerous and demanding circuit in the world. For decades it served as the ultimate proving ground for every major manufacturer on earth. Porsche, Ferrari, Mercedes, Lamborghini, all of them have used the Nordschleife to validate their engineering against something no laboratory can replicate: 20 kilometres of honest, unforgiving road. In Gran Turismo 7, that history becomes something I can feel. To set a good lap there in the game requires more than raw speed. It requires understanding the vehicle: how each corner flows into the next, where the elevation changes unsettle the car, where to brake earlier than instinct wants, and where a small mistake can ruin an entire lap. It feels endless, demanding, and alive in a way no other circuit does for me. That is why it is the only track that has ever fully held my attention.

If financial circumstances had been different, I would have fought for a path as a professional driver. The same disciplines I pursue today would still define me, physics, music, programming, and intellectual inquiry, but racing would have been part of that life too. If I had pursued it, Porsche would have been the direction.

As with everything else, the constraint is time. These games do not get played casually or on impulse. They get played when a window opens in a day already filled with work, study, music, and everything else that demands attention. That scarcity does not diminish the experience. If anything, it sharpens it.
