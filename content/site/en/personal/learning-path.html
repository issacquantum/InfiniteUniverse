Childhood Questions

This section is about my learning path, not only school. It explains how questions, language, music, computing, independent study, and this site became the real structure of my education.

My education began long before any classroom. My father is a deeply religious man, and when I was young he often told my brother and me biblical stories. I do not remember the exact questions I asked, but I remember that those stories brought the word God into my world. When I asked what God was, my father described God as something with no beginning and no end. That was one of the first times the idea of infinity entered my mind. I did not yet have the language to connect it to existence, time, or the universe, but the question stayed there. God did not feel real to me in the same way that being alive felt real, so I kept that doubt secret and began trying to understand existence on my own.

School, English, and Independent Study

My first experience with formal education was kindergarten, where I quickly grew bored. The pace of the classroom felt too slow for the questions and interests I was already carrying. Things did not improve at the public elementary school I attended before my parents separated.

After my parents separated, my father, despite serious financial hardship, found a way to enroll my brother and me in Instituto Valle, a private institution. The academic environment was more challenging and more suited to how my mind worked. It was there that I learned to speak English, and where my favourite subjects, music, English, and mathematics, began to take shape. It was also where my obsession with computing began. My first computing class introduced me to computers as systems of precise input, precise output, and structure, and I immediately felt pulled toward that logic. Outside of school, something else was quietly happening. My father kept a small encyclopedia at home. One day I picked up a book at random, drawn only by its color, and it turned out to be an astronomy book, filled with images of planets and outer space. I read sections I did not fully understand, encountering ideas far beyond my age. Because my father's worldview was religious, I pursued that curiosity quietly, conducting my own research without anyone in my family knowing. That was one of the first times education became something I did for myself, outside permission and outside the classroom.

Music as Independent Study

Music became one of my strongest forms of independent education. It trained the same habit that later shaped mathematics, programming, and physics: I learned by searching, listening, repeating, correcting, and trusting private curiosity more than formal instruction.

Formal Schooling and Leaving IPN (Instituto Politécnico Nacional)

After middle school came preparatory school, where I enrolled at IPN (Instituto Politécnico Nacional), directed toward industrial engineering, though not entirely by my own choosing. My father steered that decision. The only classes I genuinely attended were mathematics, trigonometry, calculus, and informatics, and even then, attendance was not driven by the institution but by the rare individuals within it. The trigonometry teacher, who also taught mathematics and calculus, was the single educator there who demonstrated real interest in teaching, someone who made the material feel alive and worth showing up for. The informatics teacher was inconsistent at best, frequently absent without explanation, making it difficult to take the subject seriously regardless of my interest in computing. The rest of the curriculum offered nothing that challenged me. The classes were not difficult. The environment was not stimulating. I was not happy there, the truth is that even if financial necessity had not eventually forced me to leave, I would have left on my own terms. The school was not the right place for the way my mind worked, and I knew it early. Eventually, financial necessity made the decision formal, and I left IPN before completing my studies. Outside the institution, my independent study continued moving faster than the curriculum around me. By then, I had already learned that the subjects that shaped me most would have to be pursued alone.

Independent Learning Pattern

Throughout all of it, one pattern has remained constant: I have always learned on my own terms, driven by genuine curiosity rather than institutional structure. Formal education, as it is commonly designed, rarely kept pace with the questions I was already carrying. The subjects that shaped me most were the ones I pursued privately, independently, and without permission. That has never changed. My education is not just a list of schools I attended. It is the record of how I learned to teach myself, how I learned to trust curiosity over permission, and how that habit became the foundation for music, technology, and the intellectual work I continue building today.

Programming Education and This Site

My programming education has not followed a conventional path. I study through freeCodeCamp, Eric Matthes's Python Crash Course, 3rd Edition: A Hands-On, Project-Based Introduction to Programming, multiple Udemy courses on HTML, CSS, Java, and Python, modern interactive learning tools, and Harvard CS50x 2024 and Harvard CS50x 2026, taught by Professor David J. Malan. I knew I wanted to create a website dedicated to myself and to specific people central to me when I was 25, and the idea has been taking shape little by little ever since. Earlier versions were limited by the computer I had at the time and by how much I still needed to learn about programming. Because time is limited, this website became the main place where I put programming into practice as I learn it. I do not always have time to test every concept in separate side projects, so the project itself became the laboratory.
