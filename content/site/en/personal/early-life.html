I was born in Mexico City as Raul Issac Tabares Mendoza, the son of Raul Tabares Torres and Margarita Mendoza Lopez. I grew up with my younger brother, Ismael Isaias Tabares Mendoza, in a small room inside a tenement where several members of my mother's family also lived. The space was tiny, almost a complete home compressed into a single room, but it was there that many of the earliest parts of my identity began to form.

My childhood was shaped by complexity, limitation, and contrast. There was chaos around me, but there was also intelligence, discipline, and survival. My father has played a central role in my intellectual development. He taught my brother and me to read when we were very young, giving us a foundation that stayed with me for the rest of my life. Reading was not just an academic skill; it became one of the first ways I learned to access worlds beyond my immediate circumstances.

My mother built on that foundation in a way that became unforgettable. When I was around five or six years old, she told my brother and me that if we cleaned the small room we lived in, and if I read a section of an adult book and explained what I understood when she came home from work, she would buy us a PlayStation. I do not remember the title of the book or the exact pages I read, but I remember the challenge. Later in life, my mother admitted that she had not expected me to understand what I read. When she came home and realized that I could explain it, she paused, left the house late that night, and came back with our first PlayStation 1.

That moment changed my life in ways none of us could have predicted. What began as a reward for reading and discipline became my first deep encounter with digital systems, competition, reflexes, pattern recognition, and virtual worlds. Through that PlayStation, my obsession with Tekken and Gran Turismo began. Those games were not just entertainment to me. They became worlds of mastery, precision, repetition, and identity. Tekken, especially, became one of the earliest places where I understood the feeling of serious competition.

My mother has also been one of my earliest connections to music. Music matters deeply to her and she plays guitar, and for years she tried to encourage me to play. I refused at first, not realizing what I was missing. Around 15 or 16, after learning on a schoolmate's guitar, I already knew how strongly the instrument mattered to me. My mother then gave me my first guitar, a second-hand instrument that became important immediately. Later, my father bought me my first electric guitar, a Dean VMNT (Dave Mustaine VMNT signature guitar model) United Abominations model connected to Megadeth. I no longer have it, but it remains my favourite electric guitar. In that sense, my parents gave me objects that became central to my life: my first PlayStation and my first guitar.

My father is, and has been, one of the largest influences in my life. He has made mistakes, as all people do, but he gave me much of the structure that allowed me to get as far as I have. He enrolled me in schools that challenged me, and he gave me the expectation that I should always try my best, no matter the circumstances. He also influenced the direction that eventually led me toward physics, although in an ironic way. My interest in physics began partly as a response to his religious views, as I searched for my own way to understand reality, truth, matter, existence, and the structure of the universe.

Both of my parents have a kind of practical intelligence that I have always admired. They can disassemble engines, repair them, and put them back together. They can work with mechanical and electrical devices, not as an abstract hobby, but because necessity demanded it. That left a strong impression on me. Much of my own life has followed a similar pattern: learning because I had to, adapting because circumstances required it, and combining my interests with whatever was necessary to survive.

Their work histories were also shaped by necessity rather than comfort. Both of my parents left school because life required it, and in that sense my own interrupted education did not come from nowhere; I repeated a pattern that already existed in my family, even if the reasons and consequences were different.

My father learned metalworking and blacksmithing from my grandfather, who was also very smart. He did not learn it as a hobby. He was pushed into it by necessity, but he became capable of creating real things out of metal, a skill I do not have. He has always learned quickly and moved through different kinds of work. Before I was born, he worked as a bank cashier. Later, he spent many years at a large candy business in Central de Abastos. Today I only know the general outline of his work: he uses a company motorcycle for deliveries, including important documents and products. I do not want to describe more than I actually know.

My mother also worked across different jobs, mainly as a cashier, secretary, and in an editorial environment connected to books. She is very smart and determined when something genuinely interests her, and she has always had the ability to commit herself fully when she cares about something.

My father has also given me a strong connection to sports and vehicles. Vehicles matter deeply to him, especially motorcycles, and he has always been good at most sports, especially basketball. My mother has also always been very good at basketball. As a child, I was drawn to basketball, soccer, and football. I was not interested in sports only as something to watch; I cared about playing them. They were arenas of discipline, effort, physical intelligence, and competition. I was good at them, and even earned a basketball diploma that no one knew about until my father found it years later. That seriousness toward sports was part of a larger pattern in my life: when something mattered to me, I did not experience it casually. I wanted to understand it, perform it, and master it.

My parents separated when I was around six or seven years old, adding another layer of emotional weight to an already complex childhood. We were brought before a judge and asked who we wished to live with. Both my brother and I chose our father without hesitation. My relationship with both parents has been difficult in different ways, and over time I made decisions to distance myself from parts of my family life that could no longer remain healthy for me.

My brother, Ismael, shared my resistance to school in his own way. His strengths showed elsewhere. He became very good at skateboarding and freestyle trick cycling, the kind of movement and balance closer to skate culture than racing. I do not know his professional life in detail now because family issues have affected our relationship, so I will not invent a version I cannot verify. What I can say clearly is that my brother and my father are the two men who have carried the greatest personal weight in my life.

Looking back, many of the things that define me today were already present in those early years. Reading gave me access to thought. PlayStation introduced me to systems, competition, and digital worlds. Tekken and Gran Turismo gave shape to my obsession with mastery. Guitar and music became permanent after those first borrowed, second-hand, and electric instruments. My father has given me discipline, challenge, and the expectation that I should push myself. Both of my parents have shown me practical intelligence under pressure. The environment I grew up in was small and difficult, but it forced me to develop an inner world that was expansive, intense, and entirely my own.
