My professional career did not begin under favorable conditions. At 17, still a minor, the only work available to me was a factory job dedicated to manufacturing the disposable plastic cutlery commonly used at gatherings across Mexico. The conditions were harsh. The role required carrying heavy loads of materials repeatedly throughout the shift, and within the first week I sustained an injury. I lasted one week. That experience, brief and painful as it was, crystallized something important in me: I would never return to physical labor. Not out of pride, but out of a clear understanding that my mind was the tool I needed to invest in, not my body.

At 18, I relocated to Querétaro and began working at a Skechers retail store as a salesman. I was good at it. I moved up to assistant manager within a relatively short period of time. But the most consequential thing that happened at that job had nothing to do with footwear. A customer came in, and during our interaction he noticed I spoke fluent English. That small detail changed the direction of my career entirely. He connected me with a contact of his who was looking to fill an IT (information technology) position. I interviewed, and I got the job.

What I had not fully recognized until that moment was how deeply my years of self-directed interest in technology had prepared me. Computing, software, hardware, and the logic of how systems work were not things I had studied formally. They were things I had always been drawn to, explored independently, and absorbed naturally. That foundation proved to be entirely sufficient. From that first IT role, I have never left the field. Every position since has been within the technology sector, and each one has pushed me further.

Over the years my career has evolved considerably. I moved from general IT support into data analysis, and from there into systems engineering and data and systems integration. I currently work as a Data and Systems Integration Tier 2 Analyst at BlackLine, operating at the intersection of enterprise software, financial systems, and technical problem solving. The role demands precision, analytical thinking, and a thorough understanding of how complex systems communicate and fail, all of which align naturally with how I have always approached any problem placed in front of me.

Professionally, that same pattern has kept pushing me beyond assigned tasks. My interest in programming extends past the workplace because it helps me think with more precision. For this site and related work, the practical tools are VS Code (Visual Studio Code), HTML (HyperText Markup Language), CSS (Cascading Style Sheets), JavaScript, content structure, and interface logic. I am also studying Python, but I am not presenting it here as a core tool behind this site.

The wider scientific parts of my curiosity belong more naturally in My Work & Influences. In this section, the career point is narrower: technology became the path where I could turn self-directed curiosity into stability, professional value, and a way forward that did not depend on physical labor.

The one resource I consistently find myself short of is time. Every day carries more that I want to learn, build, improve, and create than the hours available allow. That is not a complaint. It is simply the condition of a mind that has never found a way to be interested in only one thing at a time.
