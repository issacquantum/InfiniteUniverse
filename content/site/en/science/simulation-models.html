Etymology

Simulation comes from Latin roots related to likeness or imitation. Model comes from an idea of measure, pattern, or reduced representation. A simulation is not reality itself. It is a disciplined imitation built to test, estimate, explore, or visualize behavior that may be too complex, large, small, slow, or dangerous to handle directly.

A good model is honest about what it leaves out. That honesty separates a useful simplification from a misleading animation.

Source of etymological origin: Online Etymology Dictionary: simulation; Online Etymology Dictionary: model.

Model, Method, Simulation

A model defines the variables, assumptions, equations, and boundaries. A numerical method turns those equations into computable steps. A simulation is the execution of that model over time, samples, agents, or grid points.

This distinction matters. If the model is wrong, a perfect algorithm only makes the wrong result faster. If the method is unstable, even a good model can produce nonsense.

Numerical Experiment

Modeling is older than computers, but modern computational simulation became powerful in the twentieth century when numerical methods, electronic computers, and statistical sampling began working together. Physics, weather, nuclear research, engineering, biology, economics, and machine learning all needed ways to study systems that resisted exact solutions.

Verification and Validation

Simulation needs discipline because attractive visuals can hide numerical failure. Verification asks whether the code solves the equations correctly. Validation asks whether the equations match the real system well enough for the claim being made. Uncertainty quantification measures how assumptions, parameters, and numerical error affect the result.

Chaos and Stochastic Simulation

Simulation is not only about smooth prediction. Lorenz showed that deterministic equations can be extremely sensitive to initial conditions. Stochastic simulation handles systems where randomness is part of the model, not just numerical noise.

Simulation is both powerful and dangerous. It can reveal patterns hidden from direct calculation, but it can also create false confidence when assumptions are not stated clearly.

Assumptions and Limits

Every model has a boundary. Orbital probability clouds, diffusion grids, chaotic attractors, cellular automata, agent motion, molecular dynamics, and Monte Carlo sampling teach different lessons because they simplify reality in different ways.

The hydrogen orbital model is part of this larger category. It is not a literal electron path. It is a sampled probability structure, so the motion needs explanation rather than only visual appeal.

Technical Terms Glossary

  • ▣ODE: Ordinary differential equation, where the unknown function depends on one independent variable, usually time.
  • ▣PDE: Partial differential equation, where the unknown field depends on multiple variables such as space and time.
  • ▣Boundary condition: A rule imposed at the edge of a domain, such as insulated, fixed, or wraparound behavior in the heat grid.
  • ▣Initial condition: The starting state from which the numerical evolution begins.
  • ▣Stochastic simulation: A simulation where randomness is part of the model rather than just numerical error.
