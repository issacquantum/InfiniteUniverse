Etymology

Information comes from Latin roots connected with giving form, shape, or structure. In this field, information is not the emotional meaning of a message. It is the measurable reduction of uncertainty.

Information theory studies how structure can be encoded, compressed, transmitted, corrupted, recovered, protected, and physically erased.

Source of etymological origin: Online Etymology Dictionary: information.

Shannon's Communication Theory

The modern field begins with Claude Shannon and his 1948 paper A Mathematical Theory of Communication. Shannon separated the technical problem of communication from the meaning of the message. A message could be poetry, telemetry, a password, a file, or a command. The first mathematical question is the same: how much uncertainty is removed when the message is received?

Before Shannon, Ralph Hartley had connected information with the number of distinguishable symbols in a communication system. Shannon completed the framework by adding probability, noise, entropy, coding, and channel capacity.

Bits and Uncertainty

The word bit, shortened from binary digit, was proposed by John Tukey and adopted by Shannon as the natural unit of binary information. One bit resolves a choice between two equally likely alternatives.

Error Correction

Shannon's noisy-channel theorem is one of the deepest results in engineering: reliable communication is possible through noise if the rate stays below capacity and the code is long enough. Error correction does not remove physics. It uses redundancy so the receiver can infer the most likely original message.

Hamming codes made this practical by showing how parity checks can detect and correct errors. Later code families, including LDPC (low-density parity-check) codes, moved real systems closer to Shannon's limit.

Key Figures and Breakthroughs

The line runs through Hartley, Shannon, Huffman, Hamming, Kullback, Leibler, Kolmogorov, Landauer, and many later researchers in coding theory, inference, statistical learning, cryptography, and quantum communication.

The discipline is precise. Information theory does not ask whether a message feels important. It asks what had to be removed from uncertainty for the message to become known.

Technical Terms Glossary

  • ▣Entropy rate: The average information produced per symbol by a source, especially when long streams have structure or memory.
  • ▣Typical sequence: A long message whose statistical pattern is close to the source distribution, making compression predictable.
  • ▣Parity check: A structured test bit or equation used to detect whether a received codeword contains an error.
  • ▣LDPC code: A low-density parity-check code, important because sparse parity constraints can approach Shannon's limit efficiently.
  • ▣Cross-entropy: The average coding cost paid when predictions use one distribution while the data follows another.
  • ▣Ciphertext: The encrypted version of a message, used in the section's discussion of perfect secrecy.
