Etymology

Thermodynamics means the study of heat, work, energy, and macroscopic change. Statistical mechanics explains those macroscopic facts from many microscopic degrees of freedom.

This topic matters because it is the physical basis for temperature, entropy, irreversibility, records, engines, noise, equilibrium, and the arrow of time.

Entropy and Microstates

Statistical mechanics connects entropy to the number of microscopic arrangements compatible with the same macroscopic description:

A gas spreading through a room is not mysterious because particles want disorder. It is likely because vastly more microstates correspond to the spread-out macrostate.

Equilibrium and Fluctuation

Equilibrium is not a frozen state. It is a statistical condition where macroscopic quantities become stable even while microscopic motion continues. Fluctuations remain, but for large systems they are usually tiny compared with the macroscopic averages.

Arrow of Time

The second law says entropy tends to increase in isolated macroscopic systems. Statistical mechanics explains why this is overwhelmingly likely, but the full arrow also depends on boundary conditions: the early universe appears to have started in a very special low-entropy state.

Technical Terms Glossary

  • ▣Macrostate: A large-scale description such as pressure, temperature, and volume.
  • ▣Microstate: A detailed microscopic arrangement compatible with a macrostate.
  • ▣Ensemble: A probability distribution over possible microstates.
  • ▣Free energy: Energy available to do useful work under specified constraints.
