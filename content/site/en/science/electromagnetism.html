Etymology

Electromagnetism names the unified theory of electric and magnetic phenomena. What first appeared as separate effects became one field theory: changing electric fields create magnetic effects, changing magnetic fields create electric effects, and light itself is an electromagnetic wave.

Source of etymological origin: Online Etymology Dictionary: electric; Online Etymology Dictionary: magnetic.

What the model represents: Light as coupled electric and magnetic field structure, not as a mechanical string wave.

Fields

Electromagnetism replaces action-at-a-distance with fields. An electric charge changes the field around it, and other charges respond to that field. The field has energy, momentum, wave behavior, and local dynamics.

Waves, Optics, and Matter

Interference, diffraction, polarization, reflection, refraction, antennas, circuits, motors, sensors, and wireless communication all depend on electromagnetic structure. The same theory explains visible light and radio waves; the difference is frequency and wavelength, not a different kind of physical substance.

Limits

Classical electromagnetism is not the final theory of light and matter. It does not explain blackbody radiation, the photoelectric effect, atomic spectra, or quantum emission and absorption by itself. Those failures are part of why quantum theory became necessary.

Technical Terms Glossary

  • ▣Electric field: A field describing force per unit charge.
  • ▣Magnetic field: A field associated with moving charges, currents, and changing electric fields.
  • ▣Polarization: The orientation structure of an electromagnetic wave's electric field.
  • ▣Gauge potential: A potential description whose redundancy becomes central in modern field theory.
