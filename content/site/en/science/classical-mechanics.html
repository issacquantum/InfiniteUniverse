Etymology

Mechanics comes from older words connected with machines, devices, and practical motion. In physics, classical mechanics is the disciplined study of motion before quantum mechanics and relativity become necessary.

It is not obsolete. It is the working language behind projectiles, planets, vehicles, structures, oscillators, control systems, simulations, and the first layer of almost every physical model.

Source of etymological origin: Online Etymology Dictionary: mechanics.

Newtonian Core

The most direct form is Newton's second law:

It says that force changes motion. The statement is simple, but it becomes powerful because it turns a physical cause into a differential equation. Given forces and initial conditions, the motion can be predicted within the domain where classical assumptions hold.

Conservation Laws

Classical mechanics is deeper than force diagrams. Momentum, angular momentum, and energy reveal what remains stable when a system changes. Conservation laws are not just bookkeeping. They are tied to symmetries: translation symmetry gives momentum conservation, rotation symmetry gives angular momentum conservation, and time-translation symmetry gives energy conservation.

Lagrangian and Hamiltonian Views

The Lagrangian view organizes motion through the action:

The actual path is the one that makes the action stationary. This is not only elegant; it becomes the bridge to field theory, relativity, quantum path integrals, and numerical simulation.

The Hamiltonian view shifts attention to coordinates and momenta. It makes energy, phase space, canonical structure, and later quantum mechanics easier to connect.

Limits

Classical mechanics is extremely accurate for large, slow systems where quantum discreteness and relativistic effects are negligible. It fails as a final framework when speeds approach light, when action scales approach \(\hbar\), or when fields and particles require quantum treatment.

Technical Terms Glossary

  • ▣Inertial frame: A frame where a free object moves at constant velocity unless a force acts.
  • ▣Phase space: The space of positions and momenta used to describe a system's state.
  • ▣Action: A quantity integrated over a path whose stationary value gives the equations of motion.
  • ▣Canonical momentum: Momentum defined from the Lagrangian, not always identical to simple mass times velocity.
