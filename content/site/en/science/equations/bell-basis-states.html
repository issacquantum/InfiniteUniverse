Bell Basis States

Equation

$$ |\Phi^\pm\rangle = \tfrac{1}{\sqrt{2}}\left(|00\rangle \pm |11\rangle\right),\qquad |\Psi^\pm\rangle = \tfrac{1}{\sqrt{2}}\left(|01\rangle \pm |10\rangle\right) $$

What the Formula Says

The Bell basis contains four two-qubit states, built from equal superpositions of paired computational basis states.

What It Means in Practice

These are the standard maximally entangled two-qubit states used in teleportation, superdense coding, and Bell tests.

Symbols

\(|\Phi^\pm\rangle\): Bell states correlating \(|00\rangle\) with \(|11\rangle\).

\(|\Psi^\pm\rangle\): Bell states correlating \(|01\rangle\) with \(|10\rangle\).

\(\tfrac{1}{\sqrt{2}}\): normalization factor.

\(\pm\): the relative phase distinguishing the plus and minus versions.

Why It Matters

Bell states are not two separate qubits with private values. They are shared states where the information belongs to the pair, making them central to quantum communication and entanglement protocols.
