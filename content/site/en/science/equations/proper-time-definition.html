Proper Time

Equation

$$d\tau = \frac{1}{c}\sqrt{-ds^2}$$

What the Formula Says

d tau equals one over c times the square root of negative d s squared.

What It Means in Practice

This means the time measured by an ideal clock carried along a timelike path.

An ideal clock is a theoretical local clock that keeps perfect time along its own worldline and does not disturb the system being measured. It represents the clean limit of a real clock, not a magical device or a particular clock design.

Symbols

\(d\tau\): infinitesimal proper time.

\(ds^2\): spacetime interval using the \((-+++)\) sign convention.

\(c\): speed of light.

Why It Matters

Proper time is local and path dependent. Different observers can disagree about coordinate time, but each clock measures its own proper time along its own worldline.

Real clocks approximate this idea when they are well built and local effects such as acceleration, temperature, vibration, and engineering error are controlled. The concept lets relativity separate the geometry of a path from the imperfections of an actual instrument.
