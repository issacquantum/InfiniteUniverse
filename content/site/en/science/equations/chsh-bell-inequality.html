CHSH (Clauser-Horne-Shimony-Holt) Bell Inequality

Equation

$$ |S| = \left|E(a,b) - E(a,b') + E(a',b) + E(a',b')\right| \leq 2 $$

What the Formula Says

The absolute value of S equals the absolute value of E of a comma b minus E of a comma b prime plus E of a prime comma b plus E of a prime comma b prime, and it is less than or equal to two.

What It Means in Practice

This means the CHSH combination of correlation functions cannot exceed two for any local hidden-variable theory.

Symbols

\(S\): the CHSH combination built from four correlation measurements.

\(E(a,b)\): the correlation measured when observer A uses setting \(a\) and observer B uses setting \(b\).

\(a, a'\): two possible measurement settings on side A.

\(b, b'\): two possible measurement settings on side B.

\(\leq 2\): the upper bound that any local hidden-variable model must obey.

Why It Matters

This inequality is the experimental hinge between Einstein's hidden-variable picture and quantum mechanics. If nature obeyed local hidden variables, the correlations would stay at or below two. Entangled quantum states can violate that limit, reaching as high as \(2\sqrt{2}\). When experiments exceed two, the classical local explanation fails. That failure is called Bell nonlocality: the joint statistics cannot be reduced to pre-existing local instructions, even though the outcomes still cannot be controlled to send a faster-than-light message.
