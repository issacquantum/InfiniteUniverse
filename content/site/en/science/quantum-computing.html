Etymology

The word quantum derives from the Latin quantus, meaning "how great" or "how much." It entered scientific vocabulary in 1900 when physicist Max Planck used the term to describe the smallest discrete unit of energy that could be emitted or absorbed, a fundamental "packet" of energy he called a Energieelement, later formalized as a quantum. The plural, quanta, became central to the theoretical framework that would eventually be known as quantum mechanics.

The word computing traces back to the Latin verb computare, formed from com- ("together") and putare ("to reckon, to settle, to prune"). In its original sense, computare meant "to sum up" or "to calculate." The term evolved through Old French into Middle English, and by the mid-20th century, computing had become synonymous with the operation of electronic machines capable of executing algorithmic instructions.

The compound term quantum computing emerged in the early 1980s, when physicists Richard Feynman and Yuri Manin independently proposed that computational systems governed by quantum mechanical principles, superposition, entanglement, and interference, could represent physical phenomena that classical computers could not efficiently handle. The pairing of the two words thus describes a paradigm of computation built not on classical binary logic, but on the discrete, probabilistic behavior of matter at the subatomic scale.

That distinction matters because quantum computing is not simply a faster version of ordinary computing. It is a different computational framework with different resources, different limits, and different engineering problems. The challenge is not only to encode information in a new way, but to preserve fragile quantum relationships long enough to shape them into a useful answer before measurement destroys them.

Qubits and Superposition

Quantum computing is a computational paradigm that harnesses the principles of quantum mechanics to process information in ways inaccessible to classical machines. Its fundamental unit is the qubit, a two-level quantum system whose general state is described by the vector

Unlike a classical bit, which can only hold the value 0 or 1, a qubit exists in a coherent superposition of both values until it is measured, and this ability to simultaneously hold multiple possibilities is the first source of quantum computational power. When several qubits are combined, the state space grows exponentially: \(n\) qubits occupy a Hilbert space of dimension \(2^n\), allowing the parallel representation and manipulation of an amount of information that scales far faster than in any equivalent classical system. The essential point is not a picture of the qubit, but its amplitudes, relative phase, and measurement basis.

Entanglement and Measurement

The second fundamental resource is entanglement, a purely quantum correlation between qubits that admits no classical description. A canonical example is the Bell state

in which measuring one qubit instantly determines the outcome of the other regardless of the distance separating them. This property, which Einstein famously called "spooky action at a distance," is today an operational resource: without entanglement there would be no quantum teleportation, no superdense coding, and none of the algorithmic advantage this technology offers. Measurement, in turn, is an irreversible act that projects the state onto one of the eigenstates of the observable and returns a classical outcome with probability \(|\alpha|^2\) or \(|\beta|^2\) according to the Born rule; after measurement the superposition is destroyed and the system is left in the state corresponding to the obtained value. This collapse imposes an essential constraint on quantum algorithm design, since all the richness of the computation must be structured so that, at the moment of measurement, amplitudes interfere constructively on the correct answer and destructively on the incorrect ones.

Quantum Gates and Circuits

Qubit manipulation is performed through quantum gates, which are unitary operations represented by matrices. The Hadamard gate

transforms \(|0\rangle\) into \(\frac{|0\rangle + |1\rangle}{\sqrt{2}}\) and \(|1\rangle\) into \(\frac{|0\rangle - |1\rangle}{\sqrt{2}}\), generating uniform superpositions and serving as the starting point of nearly every algorithm. The Pauli gates \(X\), \(Y\), and \(Z\) transform the qubit state in distinct ways, while the phase gates \(S\) and \(T\) introduce controlled phase shifts that are indispensable for achieving universality. On two qubits, the CNOT (controlled-NOT (logical negation)) gate flips the target qubit only when the control qubit is \(|1\rangle\), and it is the ingredient that, combined with single-qubit gates, generates entanglement and proves that circuit-based quantum computation can be universal. A quantum circuit is essentially an ordered sequence of these gates applied to a register of initialized qubits, followed by final measurements that extract the classical result.

Every ideal quantum gate must preserve the total probability of the state. Mathematically, this means the gate is unitary:

This condition makes circuit evolution reversible before measurement: amplitudes can rotate, acquire phase, and interfere, but their total norm cannot leak away in an ideal gate. That is also why quantum speedup should not be described as simple parallelism. The useful effect comes from arranging phases so wrong paths cancel and useful paths reinforce. A universal circuit model needs both single-qubit rotations and an entangling gate; in practice, CNOT plus a rich set of one-qubit gates is enough to approximate arbitrary quantum circuits.

From Circuits to Algorithms

Gates become algorithms when a circuit is arranged so amplitudes meet again with controlled signs. A quantum computer is not useful because it simply "tries every answer at once." It is useful because paths leading to a wrong outcome can be given opposite phases and cancel, while paths leading to a useful outcome can be aligned so they reinforce. The final measurement then becomes biased toward the answer the circuit has amplified.

Grover's algorithm is the clearest example. It begins by preparing a uniform superposition, uses an oracle to flip the phase of the marked answer, and then applies an inversion-about-the-mean step. Those two reflections rotate the state toward the marked item: the desired amplitude grows, the unmarked amplitudes shrink, and after about \(\sqrt{N}\) rounds the final measurement is likely to return the target.

Shor's algorithm uses interference differently. It turns factoring into a period-finding problem, evaluates a periodic function in superposition, and then applies the quantum Fourier transform. Values consistent with the hidden period reinforce at specific frequencies, while inconsistent phases cancel. Measurement does not reveal every branch; it returns structured information that classical post-processing can convert into a factor.

Key Quantum Algorithms

On this architecture, algorithms have been built that surpass their classical counterparts in complexity. Shor's algorithm, published in 1994, factors integers in polynomial time

while the best known classical methods require subexponential time; this difference directly threatens public-key cryptography based on RSA (Rivest-Shamir-Adleman) and elliptic curves, and is the primary driver of the global transition toward post-quantum cryptography. Grover's algorithm, in turn, searches for a marked element in an unstructured list of \(N\) entries in

queries, offering a quadratic speedup over the classical \(\mathcal{O}(N)\) sweep and applicable to a wide family of search and optimization problems. To these are added quantum algorithms for chemistry and materials, variational methods such as VQE (variational quantum eigensolver) and QAOA (quantum approximate optimization algorithm) designed for current-era hardware, and phase estimation and linear-system solvers like HHL (Harrow-Hassidim-Lloyd), which promise advantages in machine learning and data science.

Two mechanisms are especially important inside those algorithms. Phase estimation extracts hidden periodic structure and is the engine behind Shor's factoring speedup. Amplitude amplification, the core of Grover's algorithm, repeatedly reflects amplitudes so the marked answer grows while unmarked answers shrink. In both cases, the computer is not reading every branch; it is engineering interference so the final measurement is biased toward useful information.

This is also the point at which the public conversation often becomes misleading. Quantum advantage is not a universal speedup applied to every problem. It appears only when a problem can be encoded so that interference, entanglement, and measurement structure the search space more efficiently than a classical method can. That is why a handful of algorithm families matter so much: they reveal where quantum computation changes complexity and where it does not.

Ewin Tang and Dequantization

Ewin Tang, recipient of the 2025 Maryam Mirzakhani New Frontiers Prize, helped force a more disciplined standard for claims of quantum advantage. The Breakthrough Prize citation credits her with developing classical analogs of quantum algorithms for machine learning and linear algebra, and with advances in quantum machine learning on quantum data. Her work corrected a common methodological weakness: treating a speedup as purely quantum before checking whether the input model, data access, or classical baseline already contains most of the power.

Tang's senior thesis was supervised by Scott Aaronson. In her later account, Aaronson gave her several candidate problems, and she chose the Kerenidis-Prakash recommendation-systems algorithm, a Netflix-style low-rank matrix problem presented as an exponential quantum machine-learning speedup. The original technical target was a classical lower bound for that specific problem. Tang instead constructed a quantum-inspired classical algorithm under comparable sampling and query-access assumptions, forcing the comparison to track the actual resources: state preparation, access model, runtime, output format, and the strongest available classical baseline.

The central idea behind Tang's early work is dequantization. To dequantize a quantum algorithm is to rebuild the claimed speedup in a matched classical model and isolate which resource actually creates the advantage. For classical data, that means counting the cost of loading the matrix or vector, the sampling access being assumed, the precision demanded, and the form of the output. A quantum advantage is scientifically stronger when it survives that full accounting.

Technical Terms Glossary

  • ▣CNOT gate: A two-qubit controlled-NOT gate. It flips the target qubit only when the control qubit is \(|1\rangle\), and it is the standard entangling gate used in universal circuit sets.
  • ▣Rich set of one-qubit gates: A sufficiently expressive collection of single-qubit rotations and phase gates, usually combined with an entangling gate such as CNOT for universality.
  • ▣Oracle: A black-box operation assumed to answer a specific query in one step.
  • ▣Access model: The assumed way an algorithm can read, query, sample, or load its input data; changing it can change the claimed speedup.
  • ▣Classical baseline: The strongest classical method used for comparison against a quantum algorithm.
  • ▣Block-encoding: A technique that embeds a matrix inside a larger unitary operation so a quantum algorithm can transform it.
  • ▣Singular value: A nonnegative scale factor describing how a matrix stretches vectors along special directions.
  • ▣Sketching: A randomized classical technique that compresses a matrix or dataset while preserving enough structure for a target computation.
  • ▣Sublinear algorithm: An algorithm that avoids reading the entire input, possible only under specific access assumptions.
  • ▣Physical qubit: A real hardware qubit exposed to noise, control error, and decoherence.
  • ▣Logical qubit: An error-protected qubit encoded across many physical qubits.
  • ▣Fault-tolerance threshold: The physical error rate below which error correction can suppress logical errors by adding enough overhead.
  • ▣Boson sampling: A restricted photonic sampling task used to test quantum devices, not a general-purpose computing model.
  • ▣Majorana mode: A special quasiparticle-like excitation pursued in some topological quantum computing proposals.

The Fine Print: State Preparation

Many quantum machine-learning algorithms begin by assuming that a classical vector \(x\in\mathbb{C}^n\) can be loaded as an amplitude-encoded quantum state,

Equation explanation: \(x_i\) is the \(i\)-th entry of the classical vector, \(|i\rangle\) is the computational-basis state labeled by index \(i\), and \(\|x\|\) normalizes the amplitudes so the total probability is one. This notation is compact, but the physical operation is not free. If the data originally lives in a classical database, then a quantum computer needs some procedure, often imagined through QRAM (quantum random access memory) or a related state-preparation oracle, to build \(|x\rangle\) quickly. If that loading step is assumed to take only \(\mathrm{polylog}(n)\) time, then the quantum runtime can look exponentially better than a classical runtime that has to inspect the data directly.

Tang's point is that this comparison can be unfair. A classical algorithm can also be given structured access to the data. In the sample-and-query model, the algorithm can query selected entries and sample rows or columns with probabilities proportional to squared norms. This is the classical counterpart of the way quantum amplitudes naturally weight measurement probabilities. It does not represent ordinary raw access to arbitrary data, but it is a cleaner comparison against quantum algorithms that assume efficient state preparation.

A classical algorithm can sample a row \(i\) with probability proportional to \(\|A_{i,*}\|_2^2\), then sample a column \(j\) inside that row with probability proportional to \(|A_{ij}|^2\). Measuring

measuring this state in the computational basis produces exactly the same distribution \(|x_i|^2/\|x\|_2^2\). Equation explanation: this is the same amplitude-encoded vector as above, but written with the explicit \(\ell^2\) norm. The denominator normalizes the state, and the measurement rule squares the amplitudes, which is why quantum measurement and classical length-square sampling line up. So if a quantum algorithm assumes that \(|x\rangle\) can be prepared efficiently from classical data, the fair classical comparison is not always plain entry-by-entry access. It may be a classical data structure supporting \(\ell^2\)-norm sampling. That is the move that made Tang's result possible.

This access model is powerful but restrictive. It is powerful because it supports randomized numerical linear algebra: estimating inner products, sketching low-rank structure, and sampling from approximate matrix products in time that can be sublinear or even independent of the ambient dimension. It is restrictive because the data must be stored or maintained in a special way. If real data cannot support this access cheaply, then the dequantized algorithm may not be practical. If both the quantum and classical algorithms assume comparable access, however, then the claimed exponential quantum advantage must be judged under that fair comparison.

The Recommendation-Systems Result

Tang's 2018 preprint, published at STOC (Symposium on Theory of Computing) 2019 as A quantum-inspired classical algorithm for recommendation systems, answered a specific claim. Kerenidis and Prakash had given a quantum recommendation algorithm that was treated as one of the strongest candidates for an exponential quantum speedup in machine learning. The task can be framed using an \(m\times n\) preference matrix, where the goal is to sample useful recommendations from a low-rank approximation of that matrix.

That destroys the claimed exponential separation for that particular recommendation problem.

The mathematical mechanism is elegant. Quantum algorithms manipulate amplitudes; Tang's classical algorithm manipulates squared-length sampling distributions. A quantum measurement samples index \(i\) with probability proportional to \(|x_i|^2\). A classical length-square sampler does the analogous thing without needing a quantum state. Once that analogy is made precise, tools from randomized numerical linear algebra can reproduce part of the behavior that had been treated as uniquely quantum.

Quantum PCA (principal component analysis), Clustering, and Linear Algebra

Tang extended the same logic in Quantum principal component analysis only achieves an exponential speedup because of its state preparation assumptions, published in Physical Review Letters in 2021. The target there was quantum principal component analysis and nearest-centroid clustering. Again, the lesson was not that the quantum algorithm was wrong. The lesson was that the claimed exponential speedup depended on how the input data was provided.

Her later work with collaborators broadened the framework. Quantum-inspired low-rank stochastic regression showed how length-square sampling could mimic aspects of quantum matrix inversion for low-rank matrices. The sampling-based low-rank matrix arithmetic framework generalized this direction and connected dequantization to quantum singular value transformation. In 2024, work with Ainesh Bakshi improved classical singular value transformation for quantum machine learning, showing that even some very modern quantum linear-algebra frameworks can have close classical counterparts on structured low-rank inputs.

It also keeps the same disciplined theme as her dequantization work: quantum structure is real, but claims about quantum power must track temperature, access model, locality, sample complexity, and classical baselines with precision.

The tomography papers fit the same pattern. Full quantum state tomography tries to reconstruct \(\rho\) itself, which is usually too expensive at large dimension. Tang's later work asks when one can learn less but still learn the scientifically relevant part: spectra of unentangled states, reductions from mixed-state tomography to pure-state tomography, or properties of a unitary without reconstructing everything. The connection to her Gibbs-state papers is technical: both are about extracting real structure from quantum states without pretending that the entire Hilbert space is cheaply accessible.

Scientific Scope of the Result

Tang's work does not refute quantum advantage in general. Shor's algorithm, quantum simulation, quantum chemistry, cryptographic complexity, and algorithms on genuinely quantum data remain serious. Her work also does not prove that quantum machine learning can never produce a major advantage. It shows something more precise: some claimed exponential speedups disappear when the classical baseline is strengthened to match the quantum input assumptions.

The scientific value is methodological. The comparison must separate genuine quantum resources from access-model artifacts. A claimed advantage must survive the strongest classical challenge available. If a quantum algorithm only wins because it hides the cost of loading classical data, then the result is not a reliable claim about practical superiority. If it still wins after Tang-style scrutiny, the claim becomes stronger.

The operational standard is simple: compare models honestly, count the input assumptions, define the output, and then decide whether the advantage is real.

Decoherence and Error Correction

The main obstacle to realizing these promises is decoherence: the unavoidable interaction between qubits and their environment destroys the relative phases and, with them, the superposition and entanglement that sustain the computation. To this are added errors in the gates themselves and in measurements, which means that any deep circuit accumulates noise until the result becomes unreliable. Quantum error correction solves this problem by encoding one logical qubit into many physical qubits through schemes such as the Shor code, the Steane code, and especially the surface code, which offers tolerable error thresholds and a geometry compatible with planar chips. The fault-tolerance theorems guarantee that, below a certain physical error threshold, fidelity can be amplified arbitrarily at the cost of significant qubit overhead, which explains why fault-tolerant processors will require millions of physical qubits to support a few thousand useful logical qubits.

Hardware Approaches

On the hardware front, several competing approaches coexist. Superconducting qubits, based on Josephson junctions and operated at millikelvin temperatures, are the bet of IBM (International Business Machines), Google, and Rigetti, offering gate times on the order of tens of nanoseconds, although with relatively short coherence. Trapped ions, used by IonQ and Quantinuum, encode the qubit in electronic levels of ions confined by electromagnetic fields and manipulated with lasers; their fidelities and coherence times are outstanding, at the cost of slower operations. Photonic processors, developed by PsiQuantum and Xanadu, take advantage of light states to transmit and process information, with the natural advantage of operating at room temperature and integrating with quantum communication networks. Topological approaches, explored by Microsoft through Majorana modes, pursue qubits intrinsically protected against noise by the system's topology, although their experimental demonstration is still in progress. To these families are added neutral atoms, nitrogen-vacancy centers in diamond, silicon spin qubits, and hybrid proposals, each with its own balance between scalability, fidelity, and connectivity.

Current State and Limitations

The current state of the field sits in what John Preskill termed the NISQ (noisy intermediate-scale quantum) era, characterized by noisy devices of intermediate scale with tens to a few thousand physical qubits without error correction. Milestones such as the "quantum supremacy" announced by Google in 2019 with the Sycamore processor, or subsequent advances in boson sampling and narrow benchmark tasks, have demonstrated that certain narrowly designed tasks already surpass classical supercomputers, but a useful and reproducible "quantum advantage" for relevant industrial problems does not yet exist. Current limitations include restricted qubit connectivity, the maximum circuit depth before noise dominates, the bottleneck of classical readout, cryogenic and control costs, and a shortage of mature talent and software. Even so, the growing maturity of cloud platforms, the standardization of languages such as Qiskit, Cirq, and PennyLane, and sustained advances in fidelity and qubit count suggest a plausible path toward fault-tolerant processors within the next decade.

The gap between proof of principle and broad practical deployment is therefore still large. Current machines are valuable because they let researchers learn how quantum control behaves outside ideal mathematics, how noise scales in real architectures, and which algorithmic ideas survive contact with hardware. In that sense, the field is no longer speculative, but it is not yet mature enough to justify the inflated claim that classical computing is about to be replaced.

Viewed as a whole, quantum computing rests on a delicate balance between the fragility of quantum states and the power those same states offer when controlled with precision: superposition and entanglement expand the computational space, unitary gates traverse it through directed interference, algorithms transform that interference into meaningful answers, and error correction protects the entire scaffolding against the inevitable noise of the physical world. Each generation of hardware brings us closer to the threshold at which this combination will cease to be a theoretical promise and become a tool of everyday impact in cryptography, chemistry, materials, optimization, and artificial intelligence, redefining in the process what we understand as the limits of computation.
