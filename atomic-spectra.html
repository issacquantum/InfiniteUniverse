Atomic Spectra

When atoms are excited, for example by heating or electrical discharge, they emit light at specific, well-defined wavelengths. Instead of producing a continuous spectrum, they generate a set of discrete lines known as an atomic spectrum. Each chemical element has its own unique pattern, which acts like a fingerprint.

These spectral lines were studied in detail for hydrogen, the simplest atom. Experimentally, their wavelengths follow the Rydberg formula: $$ \frac{1}{\lambda} = R \left( \frac{1}{n^2} - \frac{1}{m^2} \right), \qquad m > n, $$ where $R$ is the Rydberg constant. In simple terms, this equation predicts the exact wavelengths of emitted light using two integers. This strongly suggests that the energy of the atom is not continuous, but restricted to specific levels.

In 1913, Niels Bohr proposed a model of the hydrogen atom that explains these discrete spectra by introducing quantized electron orbits. His model is based on three key postulates.

First, the electron moves in certain allowed circular orbits around the nucleus without radiating energy. These are called stationary states, and each one corresponds to a definite energy.

Second, the angular momentum of the electron is quantized: $$ L = n\hbar, \qquad n = 1, 2, 3, \dots $$ In simple terms, the electron can only occupy orbits where its angular momentum takes specific discrete values, rather than any arbitrary value.

Third, radiation is emitted or absorbed only when the electron transitions between these allowed states. The frequency of the emitted or absorbed light is determined by the energy difference: $$ h\nu = E_m - E_n. $$ This means that light is produced when the electron jumps between energy levels, and the frequency directly reflects the size of that energy change.

From these assumptions, Bohr derived the allowed energy levels for hydrogen: $$ E_n = -\frac{13.6\ \text{eV}}{n^2}. $$ In simple terms, the electron can only have certain fixed energies, labeled by the integer $n$. The negative sign indicates that the electron is bound to the nucleus, and as $n$ increases, the energy approaches zero, meaning the electron becomes less tightly bound.

When an electron falls from a higher level $m$ to a lower level $n$, it emits a photon whose wavelength matches the observed spectral lines. This explains the Rydberg formula and the discrete nature of atomic spectra.

The Bohr model successfully explains the hydrogen spectrum and introduces quantization in a concrete way. However, it has limitations. It does not accurately describe more complex atoms, and it cannot account for finer details such as spectral line splitting or the full dynamics of electrons.

Despite these limitations, Bohr’s model marked a crucial step toward modern quantum mechanics by showing that atomic structure is inherently quantized and that energy levels are discrete rather than continuous.

This interactive model visualizes the Bohr model of the hydrogen atom, specifically demonstrating how electrons transition between discrete energy levels and emit photons.

The central sphere represents the nucleus, and the concentric rings are the quantized electron orbits (or stationary states) with energy levels $n = 1, 2, 3, \dots$. When you select an "Initial State ($m$)" and a "Final State ($n$)," an electron (represented by a small white sphere) is shown making a jump from the higher energy level ($m$) to the lower energy level ($n$).

During this transition, a photon is emitted, depicted as a small wave packet. The color of this photon is determined by its energy, which in turn depends on the energy difference between the initial and final electron states ($E_m - E_n$). Higher energy transitions (larger jumps or jumps to lower n states) result in photons with shorter wavelengths (e.g., ultraviolet or violet light), while lower energy transitions produce longer wavelengths (e.g., red light or infrared).

This simulation helps to illustrate the fundamental principle of quantization in atomic spectra: atoms only emit light at specific, discrete wavelengths because electrons can only occupy specific energy levels and make discrete jumps between them.
