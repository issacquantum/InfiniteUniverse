Photoelectric Effect

The photoelectric effect is the emission of electrons from a metal surface when it is illuminated by light with sufficiently high frequency, or equivalently, sufficiently short wavelength. The emitted electrons, called photoelectrons, can be analyzed by applying a retarding voltage that just stops them, allowing their maximum kinetic energy to be measured.

Two key observations cannot be explained by classical wave theory. First, there exists a sharp threshold frequency $\nu_0$ (or wavelength $\lambda_0$) below which no electrons are emitted, regardless of how intense the light is. Second, when $\nu \ge \nu_0$, emission occurs immediately, and the maximum kinetic energy of the electrons depends on the frequency of the light, not its intensity.

In 1905, Einstein explained these results by proposing that light is made of discrete quanta, later called photons. Each photon carries energy $$ E = h\nu, $$ which means energy is delivered in fixed packets whose size increases with frequency. Higher-frequency light therefore carries more energetic photons.

A photon also carries momentum $$ p = \frac{h}{\lambda} = \frac{h\nu}{c}, $$ meaning that even without mass, light transfers a definite amount of directional momentum when it interacts with matter.

Each material is characterized by a work function $\phi$, defined as the minimum energy required to remove an electron from the surface. This is related to the threshold frequency by $\phi = h\nu_0$.

Applying energy conservation to a single photon interacting with an electron gives $$ h\nu = \phi + K_{\max}, \qquad K_{\max} = eV_0, $$ where $K_{\max}$ is the maximum kinetic energy of the emitted electron and $V_0$ is the stopping potential. In simple terms, the photon’s energy is divided into two parts: one portion is used to free the electron, and the remainder appears as kinetic energy, which is measured through the stopping voltage.

Rearranging, $$ V_0 = \frac{h}{e}\nu - \frac{\phi}{e}. $$ This shows that the stopping potential varies linearly with frequency. The slope is $h/e$, which encodes Planck’s constant, and the intercept is $-\phi/e$, which depends on the material. Increasing the intensity at fixed frequency increases the number of emitted electrons, and therefore the current, but does not change their maximum kinetic energy because each photon carries the same energy.

The threshold condition follows directly as $\nu_0 = \phi/h$ (equivalently $\lambda_0 = hc/\phi$). In simple terms, if a photon does not carry enough energy to overcome the work function, no electron is emitted, regardless of how many photons arrive. Once the threshold is exceeded, any additional energy appears as kinetic energy.

The absence of delay in emission reflects the localized nature of energy transfer. A single photon either has enough energy to eject an electron immediately, or it does not produce emission at all.

This interpretation was confirmed experimentally by Millikan in 1916, who measured the stopping potential as a function of frequency and found the predicted linear relationship. The slope yielded a value of Planck’s constant consistent with that obtained from blackbody radiation, providing independent confirmation that light behaves as discrete energy quanta.
